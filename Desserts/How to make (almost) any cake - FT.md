# How to make (almost) any cake - FT

- 120 g fat
- 120 g sugar
- 100 g eggs (about two)
- 150 g plain flour
- 1½ tsp baking powder
- ¼ tsp salt
- 20 g thin liquid
- 80 g thick liquid

For liquid fats, whisk together fat, zest, sugar and eggs. For solid fats, like softened butter, cream with sugar for one or two minutes until lightened before adding eggs.

Sift the dry ingredients and fold in. Add thin/thick liquids. Pour into your lined tin(s) and bake at 170 °C for 25 minutes or until springy and light. (A loaf tin will take longer, about 45 minutes; cupcakes about 15 minutes.) Test by poking with a toothpick or skewer. The skewer should come out clean. Cool on a rack.

<br />

**Pointers**\
For fats, butter is good for flavour, but oil will give you a longer-lasting, more moist cake. You can use a combination of both. Using ingredients with higher fat content, such as egg yolks in place of whole eggs, and crème fraîche, double cream or sour cream will result in a more tender cake. When using a thin liquid, like juice, water or coffee, pair with a fattier, thicker liquid. Adding an acidic liquid, such as yoghurt or sour cream, will give you a faster and higher rise, while improving tenderness.

<br />

**Fun tweaks**\
Replace 50 g of the flour with ground almonds for a richer cake. This can also give a sturdier base to prevent any fruit from sinking to the bottom. You could also increase flour content by 30 g per 150 g flour used. For a chocolate cake, add 30 g cocoa powder and 20 g extra thin liquid per 150 g flour. For solid add-ins, such as chocolate chips, desiccated coconut, toasted nuts or raisins, a good starting point to go with these quantities is about 100–150 g.
